Why Microservices Aren't Always the Answer

Published March 15, 2026 · 8 min read · By Sarah Chen

The tech industry has spent the better part of a decade evangelizing microservices as the default architecture for any serious application. Conference talks, blog posts, and even job descriptions treat microservices as a mark of engineering maturity. But after leading three large-scale migrations — two successful and one spectacular failure — I've come to believe that the decision framework most teams use is fundamentally broken.

The Monolith Isn't the Enemy

Let me start with a confession: the most successful system I've ever worked on is a monolith. It serves 50 million requests per day, deploys twelve times a week, and has been in production for seven years. The team that maintains it is twelve engineers, and they ship features faster than any microservices team I've managed.

The secret isn't the architecture — it's the discipline. Clean module boundaries, comprehensive tests, and a deployment pipeline that catches regressions before they reach production. These practices work regardless of whether your code lives in one repository or fifty.

When Microservices Make Sense

There are genuine reasons to adopt microservices, but they're more specific than most people think:

  • Independent scaling requirements — When one component needs 100x the compute of another, separate services let you scale efficiently.
  • Team autonomy at scale — Beyond roughly 30-40 engineers, a shared codebase creates coordination overhead that slows everyone down.
  • Technology heterogeneity — If you genuinely need different languages or runtimes for different components (ML in Python, real-time in Rust), services are the natural boundary.
  • Compliance isolation — PCI, HIPAA, or SOC2 requirements may mandate that certain data processing happens in isolated environments.

The Hidden Costs

What the conference talks don't emphasize enough is the operational tax. Every new service means another deployment pipeline, another monitoring dashboard, another runbook, another set of alerts. Distributed tracing, service mesh configuration, contract testing — these aren't optional extras, they're table stakes for running microservices in production.

I've seen teams spend six months building platform infrastructure before shipping a single feature. That's not engineering maturity — it's yak shaving at industrial scale.

A Better Decision Framework

Instead of asking "should we use microservices?", ask these questions:

  1. What specific problem are we solving that our current architecture can't handle?
  2. Have we exhausted simpler solutions (better module boundaries, async processing, read replicas)?
  3. Do we have the operational maturity to manage distributed systems? (Honest answer required.)
  4. Can we afford the 2-3x increase in infrastructure complexity?

If you can't articulate a clear, specific answer to question one, you probably don't need microservices yet. And that's perfectly fine.
